# Mini Aquatic Ecosystems – Experiment 01

## Objective
To observe how different biological inputs affect small aquatic systems.

## Hypothesis
Systems C and D are expected to show earlier biological activity 
due to higher initial microbial load. System B will show gradual 
changes from decomposition. System A should remain unchanged.

## Sample Origin
San Miguel, Cañas, Guanacaste, Costa Rica.
Samples collected from thermal springs and creek.

## Systems
A – Control  
B – Leaf (organic input)  
C – Biofilm (microbial input)  
D – Thermal water + algae

## Setup
- Plastic containers
- Partial gas exchange (covered with perforated film)
- Indirect light conditions

## Day 17 Observations


## Preliminary Observations
Surface films appeared across all systems starting on Day 4. While early development (Day 4–6) appeared relatively stable, later observations (Day 15–16) show slow but consistent changes:

- System B shows a color shift in the surface layer (yellow/light brown)
- System C shows continued transformation of the original biofilm (progressive browning)
- System D shows increasing fragmentation, with surface material forming larger flakes and beginning to sink

These observations suggest that:

> The systems are not static, but instead undergoing slow, low-intensity transformations over time.

## Key Patterns
System A (Control): Thin, stable surface layer with minimal change over time.
System B (Leaf Input): Similar structure to A, but with a late-stage color shift in the surface layer, suggesting delayed transformation processes. No clear structural decomposition of the leaf observed.
System C (Biofilm Input): Showed the earliest and most extensive surface formation. The original biofilm continues to change color (green → brown), indicating ongoing transformation, though surface structure remains relatively stable.
System D (Thermal Water + Algae): Displays the most fragmented and heterogeneous structure. Surface material forms larger flakes and shows evidence of detachment and sinking, indicating internal redistribution of matter.

## Interpretation 
The formation of a white/translucent surface layer across all systems suggests a common process, likely related to suspended organic matter or microbial structuring at the air–water interface.
However:
- No increase in turbidity
- No odor development
- No visible decomposition (System B)
- No sustained expansion of surface structures
This indicates that the surface structure formation does not necessarily correlate with active or sustained biological processes.

## Emerging Hypothesis 1V
All systems appear to be globally limited, potentially by:
- nutrient availability
- oxygen dynamics
- or insufficient energy input (light exposure)
As a result, initial structure forms (Day 4–6), but no further system evolution occurs

## Emerging Hypothesis 2V
All systems appear to be constrained, potentially by:

- limited nutrient availability
- oxygen dynamics at the air–water interface
- or low energy input (light conditions)
  
However, observations suggest that:

> Instead of being inactive, the systems are undergoing slow structural and compositional changes, including color shifts and material redistribution.

This indicates that:

- Surface layer formation is rapid (Day 4–6)
- Subsequent changes occur at a much slower rate (Day 10+)
